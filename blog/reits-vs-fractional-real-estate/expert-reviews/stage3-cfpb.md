DOMAIN FINDINGS:

This post handles a "which to choose" comparison without recommending either, which is the right consumer-protection posture. The FAQ on "Are REITs safer than fractional" gives a balanced, multi-factor answer rather than a binary yes/no. K-1 complexity is flagged at first introduction. Tax-advisor consult lines appear in the right places.

The Opening's anti-sales framing ("written for the reader, not the salesperson") is consumer-friendly and unusual in this comparison space.

Disclaimer block matches canonical. Risk disclosure present at multiple points.

FLAGGED ISSUES:

1. **"Some investors put the majority of their real estate exposure in a low-cost REIT ETF and use fractional for a smaller, selective layer. Others reverse it."** - SEVERITY: MED - FIX: This sentence reads as portfolio-allocation guidance even though the next paragraph disclaims it. A reader who scans the section may take "majority in REIT ETF, smaller layer in fractional" as a recommendation. Either:
   (a) Remove the sentence and rely on the "no specific allocation" disclaimer below, or
   (b) Reframe as a question: "Should you put most of your real estate exposure in REITs and use fractional selectively, or reverse it? That depends on your liquidity needs..." Keeps the educational content without an implicit recommendation.

2. **"199A pass-through deduction" mentioned twice without consumer-clarity** - SEVERITY: LOW - FIX: For a non-tax-expert consumer, "199A" is jargon. Either spell out once ("the Section 199A qualified business income deduction") or describe the effect plainly ("up to 20 percent of qualifying REIT dividends may be deductible under current rules"). Current wording assumes more tax knowledge than the ICP probably has.

3. **"Common profiles" framing in choose-reit / choose-fractional** - SEVERITY: LOW - FIX: I agree with FINRA - add a "general patterns, not advice" disclaimer. Especially important given CFPB scope on consumer-directed comparative content.

4. **FAQ "How are REITs taxed vs fractional"** - SEVERITY: LOW - FIX: Good answer but it does not mention state tax variations. For a consumer in a high-tax state, state treatment can be the deciding factor. Add one sentence: "State tax treatment also varies and can be material; consult a tax advisor for your state."

RESPONSE TO PREVIOUS REVIEWERS:

I agree with SEC flag #4 (199A future-proofing) and FINRA flag #2 (interest-rate softening). Both reflect the same underlying CFPB-aligned principle: avoid stating a forward-looking or historically-grounded claim as a deterministic relationship.

I disagree with FINRA flag #1 (FAQ opener tightening). I think "no clean answer" is more honest and consumer-protection-aligned than a more confident-sounding alternative. The opener correctly disabuses the reader of the comparison they came in expecting.
