# Fractional Real Estate vs REIT: Which Is Right for You?

**Dek:** Both let you invest in real estate without buying a property outright. They work very differently. Here is the honest comparison.

[VISUAL-HERO-01]

---

If you have spent any time researching REITs, you have almost certainly bumped into fractional real estate. The two get compared constantly, usually by someone selling one or the other. This is the version written for the reader instead of the salesperson, and the whole fractional real estate vs REIT question turns out to hinge less on which is better than on which fits what you actually need.

## Quick Answer (60 seconds)

A REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust) is a pooled fund that owns many properties. You buy shares like a stock, get daily liquidity for the publicly traded ones, and receive dividend income taxed as ordinary income. Fractional real estate platforms let you own shares in a specific property through an LLC, with property-level transparency, monthly or quarterly distributions, and a multi-year hold. The core of the fractional real estate vs REIT choice is this: REITs prioritize liquidity and diversification, while fractional prioritizes control and property-specific exposure. Both involve real risk, including loss of principal.

**Quick numbers:**

  - Daily: the liquidity of a publicly traded REIT
  - 5 to 10 years: a typical fractional real estate hold period
  - Over 4%: the average REIT dividend yield in early 2026
  - $20: a per-share minimum on some fractional platforms

Past performance does not predict future results. All investments carry risk including loss of principal.

The headline difference is structural, not directional. REITs and fractional platforms are not better than each other in any absolute sense; they serve different goals. For many investors the right answer is a mix, and for others it is one or the other, depending on what they actually need from their real estate exposure. This guide lays out the differences honestly so you can decide which side, or which blend, fits you.

## What is a REIT?

A Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) is a company that owns, operates, or finances income-producing real estate. By tax rule, a REIT must pay at least 90 percent of its taxable income to shareholders as dividends, which is why REIT investing is so closely associated with steady dividend income. REITs come in three forms, and each has a different liquidity, transparency, and risk profile.

Publicly traded REITs trade like stocks on major exchanges. You buy shares through any broker, pricing is transparent, you can sell during market hours, and the dividend stream is predictable but variable. Non-traded REITs are SEC-registered but do not trade on an exchange. The SEC's investor.gov page calls out specific risks: a lack of liquidity, limited share-value transparency (non-traded REITs often do not provide a per-share value estimate until 18 months after the offering closes), distributions that are sometimes paid from offering proceeds and borrowings rather than property cash flow, and conflicts of interest with external managers. Private REITs are not registered with the SEC and are limited to accredited investors, with the least disclosure and the highest risk.

When most people say REIT, they mean the publicly traded version, and that is the default in this comparison unless noted.

## What is fractional real estate investing?

Fractional real estate investing is the practice of multiple investors co-owning a single property through shares in an LLC that holds title. You buy a unit of ownership in the LLC and receive a proportional share of rental income and any appreciation when the property is sold. Offerings are typically filed under SEC Regulation A or Regulation D, and minimums on some regulated US platforms start at $20.

The legal structure matters. You are not buying a deed; you are buying a security, a unit of membership interest in an LLC that owns the property. Distributions are typically reported on a Schedule K-1 (Form 1065) because the LLC is a pass-through entity. K-1 reporting can be more complex than a standard 1099 and may delay your tax filing, so consult a tax advisor before investing.

Some platforms, like PSFnetwork, use a per-square-foot ownership model, where your stake is expressed in square feet of the property rather than in abstract shares. The underlying legal structure is the same; what changes is the intuition, since a square foot is easier to picture than a share. This is one flavor of fractional ownership real estate, and it sits alongside the more common share-based platforms rather than replacing them.

## What are the key differences between REITs and fractional real estate?

The five biggest differences are liquidity, control, tax treatment, diversification, and transparency. REITs offer daily liquidity, no property-level control, ordinary-income dividend taxation, built-in diversification across many properties, and standardized SEC disclosure. Fractional offers a multi-year hold with a limited secondary market, the ability to pick specific properties, pass-through K-1 taxation, single-property exposure unless you aggregate, and offering-level disclosure for each property.

| **Dimension** | **Publicly traded REIT** | **Fractional platform** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Liquidity | Daily, during market hours | 5 to 10 year hold; limited or no secondary market |
| Control over properties | None (managed portfolio) | Yes (pick specific properties) |
| Minimum investment | Price of one share, or smaller with fractional shares | $20 to $5,000 per share |
| Income frequency | Quarterly typical | Monthly or quarterly typical |
| Tax form | 1099-DIV (most) | K-1 (Form 1065) |
| Tax treatment | Dividends taxed as ordinary income | Pass-through; may include depreciation pass-through |
| Diversification | Built-in across many properties | Single property per investment unless aggregated |
| Disclosure | SEC 10-K, 10-Q, quarterly earnings | Offering circular per property; updates per platform |
| Interest-rate sensitivity | High (REIT prices move with rates) | Lower (private market, not daily-priced) |

Most publicly traded REITs report dividends on a 1099-DIV, though some non-traded REITs structured as partnerships may issue a K-1 instead, so check the offering documents. Returns are not guaranteed for either structure: REIT prices can fall, fractional property values can fall, and platform-reported yields are not promises. Anyone comparing fractional real estate vs REIT on returns alone is missing the more important differences in liquidity, taxes, and control.

## When should you choose a REIT?

A REIT, especially a publicly traded REIT or a REIT ETF, suits investors who prioritize liquidity, instant diversification, and brokerage-account simplicity. Common profiles include investors who hold the position inside a tax-advantaged account such as an IRA or 401(k), where the ordinary-income dividend treatment is neutralized, investors who want real estate exposure without operational complexity, and investors who may need to exit quickly if their circumstances change.

The case for REIT investing is structural. The average REIT dividend yield was over 4 percent in early 2026, roughly three times the average dividend stock, and publicly traded REITs add liquidity that no private structure can match. Whether that makes them right for you depends on your own time horizon and account type, not just on the asset.

The cost is interest-rate sensitivity. REITs have historically shown sensitivity to interest rates, with periods of underperformance when rates rise, since their dividends then compete with bond yields and their cost of capital climbs. They have outperformed at other times, when rates fall. Holding through a full rate cycle is the price of admission. Inside a taxable brokerage account, the ordinary-income tax treatment is a meaningful drag at higher marginal rates, which is why some investors hold REITs in IRAs or 401(k)s. A portion of REIT dividends may also qualify for a deduction of up to 20 percent under current US tax rules (Section 199A, which may change). Consult a tax advisor for your situation.

## When should you choose fractional real estate?

Fractional real estate suits investors who want property-level transparency, are comfortable with a multi-year hold, value K-1 pass-through tax treatment, and want exposure to specific properties rather than a portfolio average. Typical profiles include investors who already have liquid market exposure and want a different return profile, investors who want to see and choose the underlying asset, and investors at lower capital levels where fractional minimums beat a direct-ownership down payment.

The case for fractional is also structural. You see the property, the operating agreement, the fee schedule, and the offering circular before you commit, and the depreciation pass-through on the K-1 can offset some of the distribution income for tax purposes. Returns reported by platforms have historically fallen in a 4 to 10 percent annual range, though those numbers are platform-reported and not guaranteed.

The cost is illiquidity and platform risk. You cannot exit on a Tuesday afternoon, because your capital is committed for the property's hold period, often five to ten years. The platform itself can fail, and recovering full value in a failed-platform scenario can take months or years and is not assured. These profiles are general patterns, not individual investment advice.

## Can you hold both REITs and fractional real estate?

Yes, and most investors who want real estate exposure benefit from holding both, because the two structures complement each other. REITs provide liquidity, sector breadth, and a daily-priced market position. Fractional provides property-level transparency, a different correlation profile, and pass-through tax features. For most portfolios, neither is a complete real estate allocation on its own.

A simple way to think about it is that REITs are the liquid layer and fractional is the structured-private layer. Whether most of your real estate exposure should sit in REITs with fractional playing a selective role, or the reverse, depends on your liquidity needs, your tax situation, and your conviction on specific properties. This guide does not recommend a specific allocation, because the right ratio depends on your full portfolio, income, time horizon, and tax bracket. The useful takeaway is that "REIT or fractional" usually has a "both, in some ratio" answer.

## FAQ

### Q: Are REITs safer than fractional real estate?

A: Safer is the wrong frame, because each is safer on a different axis. Publicly traded REITs are more liquid, so you can exit faster, which reduces one kind of risk, but they are also more interest-rate sensitive and their daily price moves can be larger than the slow revaluation of a fractional position. Fractional carries platform risk and illiquidity, while REITs carry market volatility risk and sector concentration risk if you hold a single REIT rather than an ETF. Neither is risk-free, and past performance is not indicative of future results.

### Q: Are REITs a good investment?

A: REITs can be a good investment for the right goal, especially if you want liquid, diversified real estate exposure and can hold through interest-rate cycles. With an average dividend yield over 4 percent in early 2026 and the ability to sell during market hours, they suit investors who value flexibility. They fit less well if you want to choose a specific property or avoid daily price swings. No structure is universally a good investment, so match it to your goal and time horizon.

### Q: Which has higher returns, REITs or fractional real estate?

A: There is no clean answer. REIT total returns combine dividends, over 4 percent on average in early 2026, with price appreciation that can be negative in down years. Fractional platform-reported yields have historically fallen in a 4 to 10 percent annual range, with appreciation realized at sale. Cherry-picked comparisons can favor either side, so use ranges, account for fees and taxes, and do not extrapolate any single number.

### Q: How are REITs taxed versus fractional real estate?

A: REIT dividends are reported on a 1099-DIV (typically) and taxed as ordinary income, not at qualified-dividend rates, though a portion may qualify for the 20 percent pass-through deduction under current US tax rules. Fractional real estate distributions are typically reported on a Schedule K-1 (Form 1065) because the underlying entity is a pass-through LLC, and you may also receive depreciation pass-through that offsets some distribution income. Tax treatment varies by structure and by jurisdiction, so consult a tax advisor.

### Q: What is the minimum to invest in a REIT versus fractional?

A: For publicly traded REITs, the minimum is the price of one share, often $50 to $300, though many brokers offer fractional shares for $1 to $10. For fractional real estate platforms, per-share minimums on some regulated US platforms start at $20, while PSFnetwork sells by the square foot. Non-traded and private REITs often have higher minimums, from $1,000 to $25,000 or more, and may require accredited investor status.

### Q: Can you invest in REIT ETFs instead of individual REITs?

A: Yes, and many investors do. REIT ETFs such as VNQ or IYR hold many REITs and offer diversification at low expense ratios. The same tax treatment applies, with distributions mostly ordinary income and some portion potentially qualifying for the 199A pass-through deduction. Among the best REITs to invest in for beginners, a broad REIT ETF is often the simplest starting point because it spreads risk across the whole sector.

### Q: What is the difference between a REIT and a real estate fund?

A: A REIT is a specific legal entity that meets IRS REIT rules, including the 90 percent distribution requirement, asset tests, and ownership rules. A real estate fund is a broader term covering REITs, private real estate funds, real estate mutual funds, and similar pooled vehicles. All REITs are real estate funds, but not all real estate funds are REITs.

## Conclusion

The honest answer to fractional real estate vs REIT is that you are choosing between two different things, not ranking one above the other. A REIT gives you liquidity, broad diversification, and the simplicity of a brokerage account, at the cost of ordinary-income taxation and real sensitivity to interest rates. Fractional gives you a specific property, pass-through tax treatment, and a transparency you can read line by line, at the cost of a multi-year lockup and dependence on a single platform. If you need to be able to sell on short notice, lean toward REIT investing, ideally inside a tax-advantaged account. If you want to choose the building, can leave the money alone for years, and value seeing exactly what you own, fractional earns its place. Most investors who are serious about real estate end up holding some of each: REITs as the liquid layer, fractional as the structured-private layer that lets them pick specific assets, including per-square-foot platforms like PSFnetwork. Whichever way you lean, the discipline is the same. Read the disclosures, account for fees and taxes, respect the hold period on the private side, and remember that neither structure removes the underlying risk of owning real estate, including the possible loss of principal.
