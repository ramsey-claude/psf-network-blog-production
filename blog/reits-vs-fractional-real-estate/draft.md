# Fractional Real Estate vs REIT: Which Is Right for You?

**Dek:** Both let you invest in real estate without buying a property outright. They work very differently. Here is the honest comparison.

[VISUAL-HERO-01]

---

## Quick Answer (60 seconds)

A REIT (Real Estate Investment Trust) is a pooled fund that owns many properties; you buy shares like a stock, get daily liquidity for publicly traded ones, and receive dividend income taxed as ordinary income. Fractional real estate platforms let you own shares in a specific property through an LLC, with property-level transparency, monthly or quarterly distributions, and a multi-year hold. REITs prioritize liquidity and diversification. Fractional prioritizes control and property-specific exposure. Both involve real risk, including loss of principal.

**Stat cards:**
- **Daily** - Liquidity of a publicly traded REIT
- **5 to 10 years** - Typical fractional hold period
- **Over 4%** - Average REIT dividend yield in early 2026
- **$20** - Per-share minimum on some fractional platforms

## Opening

If you have spent time researching REITs, you have probably already met fractional real estate. The two get compared constantly, often by people selling one or the other. This is the comparison written for the reader, not the salesperson.

The headline difference is structural, not directional. REITs and fractional platforms are not "better than each other" in some absolute sense. They serve different goals. The right answer is usually a mix, or one or the other, depending on what you actually need from your real estate exposure.

---

## What is a REIT?

**Answer capsule:** A Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) is a company that owns, operates, or finances income-producing real estate. By tax rule, REITs pay at least 90 percent of taxable income to shareholders as dividends. They come in three forms: publicly traded (listed on stock exchanges), non-traded (SEC-registered but not exchange-listed), and private. Each form has a different liquidity, transparency, and risk profile.

**Publicly traded REITs** trade like stocks on major exchanges. You buy shares through any broker. Pricing is transparent, you can sell during market hours, and the dividend stream is predictable but variable.

**Non-traded REITs** are SEC-registered but do not trade on an exchange. The SEC's investor.gov page calls out specific risks: lack of liquidity, share value transparency (non-traded REITs typically do not provide a per-share value estimate until 18 months after the offering closes), distributions sometimes paid from offering proceeds and borrowings rather than property cash flow, and conflicts of interest with external managers.

**Private REITs** are not registered with the SEC and are limited to accredited investors. Disclosure is the least and risk is the highest.

When most people say "REIT" they mean the publicly traded version. That is the default in this comparison unless noted.

---

## What is fractional real estate investing?

**Answer capsule:** Fractional real estate investing is the practice of multiple investors co-owning a single property through shares in an LLC that holds title. You buy a unit of ownership in the LLC and receive a proportional share of rental income and any appreciation when the property is sold. Offerings are typically filed under SEC Regulation A or Regulation D, and minimums on some regulated US platforms start at $20.

The legal structure matters. You are not buying a deed. You are buying a security, a unit of membership interest in an LLC that owns the property. Distributions are typically reported on a Schedule K-1 (Form 1065) because the LLC is a pass-through entity. K-1 reporting can be more complex than a standard 1099 and may delay your tax filing; consult a tax advisor before investing.

Some platforms, like PSFnetwork, use a per-square-foot ownership model. Your stake is expressed in square feet of the property rather than abstract shares. The underlying legal structure is the same; what changes is intuition.

## What are the key differences between REITs and fractional real estate?

**Answer capsule:** The five biggest differences are liquidity, control, tax treatment, diversification, and transparency. REITs offer daily liquidity, no property-level control, ordinary-income dividend taxation, built-in diversification across many properties, and standardized SEC disclosure. Fractional offers a multi-year hold with limited secondary market, the ability to pick specific properties, pass-through K-1 taxation, single-property exposure unless aggregated, and offering-level disclosure for each property.

The full head-to-head:

| Dimension | Publicly traded REIT | Fractional platform |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Liquidity | Daily, during market hours | 5 to 10 year hold; limited or no secondary market |
| Control over properties | None (managed portfolio) | Yes (pick specific properties) |
| Minimum investment | Price of one share, or smaller if your broker offers fractional shares | $20 to $5,000 per share |
| Income frequency | Quarterly typical | Monthly or quarterly typical |
| Tax form | 1099-DIV* | K-1 (Form 1065) |
| Tax treatment | Dividends taxed as ordinary income | Pass-through; may include depreciation pass-through |
| Diversification | Built-in across many properties | Single property per investment unless aggregated |
| Disclosure | SEC 10-K, 10-Q, quarterly earnings | Offering circular per property; updates per platform |
| Interest-rate sensitivity | High (REIT prices move with rates) | Lower (private market, not daily-priced) |

*Most publicly traded REITs report dividends on 1099-DIV; some non-traded REITs structured as partnerships may issue K-1 instead. Check the offering documents.

Returns are not guaranteed for either structure. REIT prices can fall, fractional property values can fall, and platform-reported yields are not promises.

## When should you choose a REIT?

**Answer capsule:** A REIT (especially a publicly traded REIT or REIT ETF) suits investors who prioritize liquidity, instant diversification, and brokerage-account simplicity. Common profiles: investors who hold the position inside a tax-advantaged account (IRA, 401(k)) where ordinary-income dividend treatment is neutralized, investors who want real estate exposure without operational complexity, and investors who may need to exit quickly if their circumstances change.

The case for REITs is structural. The average REIT dividend yield was over 4 percent in early 2026, roughly three times the average dividend stock. Publicly traded REITs add liquidity that no private structure can match.

The cost is interest-rate sensitivity. REITs have historically shown sensitivity to interest rates, with periods of underperformance when rates rise as their dividends compete with bond yields and their cost of capital rises. They have at other times outperformed when rates fall. Holding through a full rate cycle is the price of admission.

Inside a taxable brokerage account, the ordinary-income tax treatment is a meaningful drag at higher marginal rates. Some investors mitigate this by holding REITs in IRAs or 401(k)s. A portion of REIT dividends may also qualify for a deduction of up to 20 percent of qualifying REIT dividends under current US tax rules (Section 199A, which may change). Consult a tax advisor for your situation.

---

## When should you choose fractional real estate?

**Answer capsule:** Fractional real estate suits investors who want property-level transparency, are comfortable with a multi-year hold, value K-1 pass-through tax treatment, and want exposure to specific properties rather than a portfolio average. Typical profiles: investors who already have liquid market exposure and want a different return profile, investors who want to see and choose the underlying asset, and investors at lower capital levels where fractional minimums beat a direct-ownership down payment.

The case for fractional is also structural. You see the property, the operating agreement, the fee schedule, and the offering circular before you commit. The depreciation pass-through on the K-1 can offset some of the distribution income for tax purposes. Returns reported by platforms have historically fallen in a 4 to 10 percent annual range, though those numbers are platform-reported and not guaranteed.

The cost is illiquidity and platform risk. You cannot exit on a Tuesday afternoon. Your capital is committed for the property's hold period, often 5 to 10 years. The platform itself can fail, and recovering full value through a failed-platform scenario can take months or years and is not assured.

These profiles are general patterns, not individual investment advice.

---

## Can you hold both REITs and fractional real estate?

**Answer capsule:** Yes, and most investors who want real estate exposure benefit from holding both. The two structures complement each other. REITs provide liquidity, sector breadth, and a daily-priced market position. Fractional provides property-level transparency, a different correlation profile, and pass-through tax features. Neither is a complete real estate allocation on its own for most portfolios.

A simple way to think about it: REITs are the liquid layer. Fractional is the structured-private layer. Should most of your real estate exposure sit in REITs and fractional play a selective role, or the reverse? That depends on your liquidity needs, tax situation, and conviction on specific properties.

This guide does not recommend a specific allocation, because the right answer depends on your full portfolio, income, time horizon, and tax bracket. The point is that the question "REIT or fractional" usually has a "both, in some ratio" answer.

## FAQ

**Q: Are REITs safer than fractional real estate?**
A: "Safer" depends on what you mean. Publicly traded REITs are more liquid; you can exit faster, which reduces one kind of risk. They are also more interest-rate sensitive and their daily price moves can be larger than the slow revaluation of a fractional position. Fractional carries platform risk and illiquidity; REITs carry market volatility risk and sector concentration risk if you hold a single REIT rather than an ETF. Neither is risk-free, and past performance is not indicative of future results.

**Q: Which has higher returns, REITs or fractional real estate?**
A: There is no clean answer. REIT total returns combine dividends (over 4 percent on average in early 2026) and price appreciation, which can be negative in down years. Fractional platform-reported yields have historically fallen in a 4 to 10 percent annual range, with appreciation realized at sale. Cherry-picked comparisons can favor either side. Use ranges, account for fees and taxes, and do not extrapolate any single number.

**Q: How are REITs taxed vs fractional real estate?**
A: REIT dividends are reported on a 1099-DIV (typically) and taxed as ordinary income, not at qualified-dividend rates. A portion may qualify for the 20 percent pass-through deduction under current US tax rules. Fractional real estate distributions are typically reported on a Schedule K-1 (Form 1065) because the underlying entity is a pass-through LLC; you may also receive depreciation pass-through that offsets some distribution income. Tax treatment varies by structure and by jurisdiction. Consult a tax advisor.

**Q: What is the minimum to invest in a REIT vs fractional?**
A: For publicly traded REITs, the minimum is the price of one share (often $50 to $300, but many brokers offer fractional shares for $1 to $10). For fractional real estate platforms, per-share minimums on some regulated US platforms start at $20. Non-traded REITs and private REITs often have higher minimums ($1,000 to $25,000+) and may require accredited investor status.

**Q: Can you invest in REIT ETFs instead of individual REITs?**
A: Yes, and many investors do. REIT ETFs (like VNQ or IYR) hold many REITs and offer diversification at low expense ratios. The same tax treatment applies: distributions are mostly ordinary income, with some portion potentially qualifying for the 199A pass-through deduction.

**Q: What is the difference between a REIT and a real estate fund?**
A: A REIT is a specific legal entity that meets IRS REIT rules (90 percent distribution requirement, asset-test requirements, ownership rules). A real estate fund is a broader term covering REITs, private real estate funds, real estate mutual funds, and similar pooled vehicles. All REITs are real estate funds; not all real estate funds are REITs.
