## The 60-second version

The platform comparison everyone wants is the return ranking. It does not exist in any honest form. Vintages differ. Fee schedules differ. Property mixes differ. Auditors do not produce a head-to-head IRR table because the inputs are not comparable, and the platforms that publish their own ranking always come out on top.

So skip the ranking. Match the platform to your situation. If you have ten dollars to start and you want to set it and forget it, Fundrise. If you have a hundred and you want to pick the specific building, Ark7 or Arrived. If you want to understand exactly what you own without a finance degree, psfnetwork. If you are blockchain-fluent and want daily liquidity inside the platform, Lofty. If you have $250 and want curated commercial deal flow, Mogul.

That is the whole guide in one paragraph. The rest is the work behind it.

The structural problem is that the reader is left without a neutral source.

## How did we compare them?

Six criteria, applied the same way to every platform. Regulatory status. Minimum investment. Income frequency. Fee structure. Liquidity model. Structural type, LLC or tokenized.

Where the data is auditable, we used the auditable source. SEC EDGAR for filings. Each platform's own offering circular for fees and minimums. Where the data is not auditable, which is the case for almost all yield and IRR claims in this category, we say so and label it "platform-reported."

We did not run a head-to-head return horse race. Not because it would be hard. Because it would be misleading. Two platforms publishing "8.4% annualized" can mean wildly different things if one is reporting a single vintage of stabilized assets and the other is reporting a portfolio-weighted figure with active development projects. Anyone who publishes the ranking is either ignoring this or hiding it.

## Which platform fits which investor?

The honest answer is that this question is upside down. The right question is which structural feature matters most to you, and the platform follows from there. Below are six common starting points and where each one lands.

*These "best for" assignments reflect structural fit and feature differences, not return rankings or individual investment advice.*

### You have ten dollars and you want to start now

Fundrise or HappyNest. Both list $10 minimums. Fundrise's Flagship Fund is a diversified private REIT structure, you are buying into a pool, not a property. HappyNest focuses on commercial real estate at the same entry point.

Be honest with yourself about what $10 buys. It buys the habit. It does not buy meaningful exposure. If your only goal is to start moving, either of these gets you moving. If your goal is to build a real position, you will be adding capital for years. The question of which platform then matters less for the entry point and more for whether the structure compounds the way you want.

### You have a hundred dollars and you want to pick the building

Ark7 at $20 per share, or Arrived at $100. Ark7 gives you single-property selection at the lowest per-share cost in the category. Monthly distributions. Secondary market opens after a one-year hold, per platform marketing.

Arrived is the brand-recognition play at $100. The differentiator is single-family rentals and short-term rentals as the specific asset category, plus a high-profile investor lineup that makes the platform feel less experimental than its newer competitors. Quarterly distributions, not monthly.

We have heard both pitched as "the best." Neither is the best. They are different. Ark7 is for the investor who wants to actively select properties at the lowest possible per-position cost. Arrived is for the investor who wants the comfort of a known brand and a specific asset focus they already understand.

### You have a hundred dollars and you want to understand what you own

This is where we put ourselves. psfnetwork is a $100 entry point with a per-square-foot ownership unit. You hold a stake in a specific number of square feet of a specific property at a specific address. The underlying legal structure is the same Reg A LLC framework the others use. The difference is presentation.

Why does presentation matter? Because the standard fractional industry talks in "shares" of an LLC. A share is an abstraction. Square feet are not. A first-time investor can hold one sentence in mind ("I own 14 square feet of a duplex on East 7th Street") and understand exactly what they bought. The same investor holding "0.0042 of an LLC interest" has to do a chain of mental math to get to the same place.

We are not the right answer for someone who wants the highest possible yield, who does not care about the math, or who wants the liquidity profile of a stock. We are the right answer for the investor whose biggest barrier is feeling like they understand what they own.

### You want daily liquidity and you are blockchain-fluent

Lofty. $50 per share, tokenized on Algorand, distributions paid daily in USDC. The marketplace runs inside the platform.

The structural caveats are real. Tokenized real-estate securities are in a regulatory area that is still being mapped. Custodial wallet mechanics add a layer that does not exist on standard Reg A platforms. The daily distribution and on-chain settlement are genuinely useful if you want them. They are also a different category of complexity than what you get on Ark7 or Arrived.

If you find on-chain mechanics intuitive, Lofty is the only platform in this guide doing what it does. If you find them stressful, you will be happier on a Reg A LLC platform.

### You have $250 and you want curated commercial

Mogul. $250 minimum at the time of writing. Curated deal flow rather than open access to a marketplace. Performance claims are platform-reported and unaudited.

Mogul is for the investor who wants a more concentrated, higher-minimum position in commercial property and who is comfortable with the platform doing the selection. The trade-off is that you are taking the platform's curation on faith.

### You want commercial focus and a low entry point

HappyNest, mentioned above at $10. Same Reg A LLC structure as most of this list, commercial focus, quarterly distributions. The same caveat about $10 entry points applies. It is a habit-builder, not a position-builder.

## How do you actually choose?

Five questions. Answer them in this order.

How much do you have to start? That cuts the list in half before you do anything else. Anyone with $10 is not on Mogul. Anyone with $250 is not constrained to Fundrise.

Do you want to pick the property, or are you happy with a managed portfolio? This split matters more than people realize. Fundrise is the diversified pool. Almost everything else is property-by-property.

How often do you want income? Lofty pays daily. Ark7 pays monthly. Most others pay quarterly. If you are reinvesting, frequency barely matters. If you want the dopamine of a distribution showing up regularly, it matters more.

Are you comfortable with blockchain mechanics? If yes, Lofty unlocks something the others do not have. If no, skip it without guilt.

What asset focus do you want? Commercial, single-family, mixed, fund-of-funds. Each platform leans different ways. The mismatch between your interest and the platform's focus is where regret comes from.

That is five questions. Your answers point to a short list of two or three platforms. From there, the only thing left is to read the offering circular of a specific live property on each one and compare the fee schedule line by line. There is no shortcut for this step. The marketing pages do not show the fees the way the offering circular does.

## What should you watch out for across every platform?

Four risks. They apply equally to whichever platform you choose, including ours.

**Platform risk.** The operator can fail. The property is held in an LLC separate from the platform, which protects you somewhat, but a failed platform means delays, disputes, and an uncertain replacement servicer. Read the operating agreement's backup-servicer language before you invest. If it does not exist, ask why.

**Illiquidity.** Five to ten years is the realistic hold period for most of these. Secondary markets exist on some platforms but they are thin. If you might need the money back in two years, this asset class is the wrong asset class for that money.

**Fees that compound.** A one percent annual management fee on a ten-year hold is not one percent. It is closer to ten percent of your starting capital, minus the compounding the fee removes. Two percent is more like twenty. Always read the fee schedule line by line. Acquisition fees, asset management fees, disposition fees, and platform fees stack. The marketing yield does not include them.

**Tax complexity.** Most LLC-structured platforms send K-1s, not 1099s. K-1s are more involved at tax time and often arrive late. Hold positions on three platforms, and your accountant will have new opinions about your hobbies.

The performance section of any marketing page is the area where the caveats matter most. Yields are labeled "target," never promised. IRRs are usually platform-reported, not third-party audited. Past performance does not predict future results. Treat any specific yield or IRR number as backward-looking and add your own margin of safety to it.

## FAQ

**Q: Are fractional real estate platforms safe?**
A: They carry real risk and are not FDIC insured. Your principal can decline. You can be unable to exit when you want. The platform can fail. Regulated platforms must disclose these risks in their offering documents. The most important safety check is to confirm the SEC filing on EDGAR before investing. If a platform is not on EDGAR, that is your answer.

**Q: Which platform has the highest returns?**
A: No one knows, because no audited apples-to-apples ranking exists. Platforms publish target or historical yields in the 4 to 9 percent range, with appreciation as an additional return component. Every one of those numbers is platform-reported. Anyone publishing a ranking is either guessing or selling you something. Diversifying across two or three platforms is a hedge against any single platform's selection bias.

**Q: Can I use multiple platforms at once?**
A: Yes, and many investors do. The benefit is spreading platform risk. The cost is administrative: separate onboarding, separate KYC, and a stack of K-1s at tax time if all the platforms are LLC-structured. If you are running multi-platform, set a calendar reminder in January for tax document chase.

**Q: What happens to my investment if a platform shuts down?**
A: The property is held in an LLC separate from the platform itself, so a platform shutdown does not automatically wipe out the underlying value. But finding a replacement servicer takes time, disputes can take months or years, and full recovery is not assured. The operating agreement's backup-servicer language is the document to read.

**Q: How are fractional real estate platforms regulated?**
A: Most US platforms file under one of two SEC rules. Regulation A Tier 2 lets non-accredited investors in with disclosure and caps the raise at $75 million per twelve months. Regulation D is typically accredited investors only. Tokenized platforms like Lofty operate in a different regulatory zone with additional considerations. Confirm the specific filing on EDGAR before committing.

**Q: How does psfnetwork compare to Arrived?**
A: Both are Reg A LLC platforms with $100 minimums. Both let you pick the specific property. The legal structure is the same. Two real differences. First, Arrived focuses on single-family and short-term rentals; ours is mixed commercial and residential. Second, ours is presented as square-foot ownership rather than abstract shares. Same underlying interest, different cognitive framing. If "I own 14 square feet of a building on East 7th Street" makes more sense to you than "I own 0.0042 of an LLC interest," you will probably prefer ours. If you do not care about the framing, Arrived's brand recognition and SFR focus is a fair reason to choose them.
