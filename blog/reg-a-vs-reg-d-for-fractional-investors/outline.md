## H2 section plan

Each section opens with a 50-75 word answer capsule (hard-count before finalizing).

1. **What is Regulation A (and what are its tiers)?**
   Capsule: Regulation A is an SEC exemption that lets companies raise capital from the public, including non-accredited investors. It has two tiers: Tier 1 allows up to $20 million per 12-month period; Tier 2 allows up to $75 million and supersedes state-level "blue sky" review but adds ongoing SEC reporting. Most fractional real estate platforms operate under Tier 2.
   Body: JOBS Act 2012 origin; the offering-circular/qualification process; ongoing semiannual reporting.

2. **What is Regulation D (and what is the 506(b) vs 506(c) distinction)?**
   Capsule: Regulation D is an SEC exemption for private placements with no offering-size limit, but with strict investor restrictions. Rule 506(b) allows up to 35 sophisticated non-accredited investors plus unlimited accredited investors, with no general solicitation. Rule 506(c) allows unlimited accredited investors with verified accreditation, and DOES permit general solicitation. Many commercial real estate syndications use 506(c).
   Body: Why Reg D dominates in real estate syndications; accreditation verification mechanics.

3. **Who is an "accredited investor" and why does it matter?**
   Capsule: An accredited investor meets specific income or net worth thresholds defined in SEC Rule 501: $1 million net worth excluding primary residence, or $200,000 annual income ($300,000 with spouse) in each of the prior two years. The category also includes certain professional credentials (Series 7, 65, 82) and entities. Reg D 506(c) and most syndications restrict access to accredited investors.
   Body: How verification works (third-party letters, tax returns, recent rule expansions for credentialed individuals).

4. **What investor protections come with each framework?**
   Capsule: Reg A requires SEC qualification of an offering circular before sales, with extensive disclosures of business, financials (audited for Tier 2), risk factors, and ongoing semiannual reporting. Reg D requires Form D filing but no SEC qualification, with disclosures driven by anti-fraud rules and accredited-investor sophistication assumptions. Reg A trades qualification overhead for retail accessibility; Reg D trades public access for offering speed.
   Body: Where each framework's disclosure ends up: Reg A in offering circular + ongoing reports; Reg D in private placement memorandum (PPM), no public SEC review.

5. **How do offering caps and timing differ?**
   Capsule: Reg A caps a 12-month rolling offering at $20 million (Tier 1) or $75 million (Tier 2). Reg D has no offering-amount limit, Rule 506 offerings can raise unlimited capital. Reg A requires SEC qualification (months of review). Reg D needs only a Form D filing post-first-sale. For a fractional real estate platform raising small recurring rounds, Reg D is faster to open but limits investor pool; Reg A is slower to open but admits everyone.
   Body: Practical timeline differences for an issuer; what this means for offering frequency on a fractional platform.

6. **Which framework do fractional real estate platforms typically use?**
   Capsule: Most consumer-facing fractional platforms operate under Regulation A Tier 2, because Tier 2 admits non-accredited investors with a per-offering cap of 10% of the greater of annual income or net worth (excluding primary residence). Larger commercial real estate syndications often use Reg D 506(c) when their target investors are accredited and the offering size exceeds the Reg A Tier 2 cap.
   Body: Examples (Fundrise, Arrived, Realbricks, psfnetwork on Reg A; CrowdStreet, larger syndicators on Reg D).

7. **How does this apply to psfnetwork specifically?**
   Capsule: psfnetwork operates under Regulation A Tier 2, which means the offering is qualified by the SEC, the offering circular and ongoing reports are publicly filed on EDGAR, and non-accredited investors can participate subject to the 10% cap. The per-square-foot model is a unit-of-ownership detail, it doesn't change the regulatory framework. The framework is what enables the consumer-facing model.
   Body: Brief; link to offering circular on EDGAR for any specific property.
