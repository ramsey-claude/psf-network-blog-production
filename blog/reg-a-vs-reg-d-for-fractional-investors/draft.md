# Reg A vs Reg D for Fractional Real Estate Investors (2026)

**Dek:** Most fractional platforms operate under one of two SEC frameworks. The choice is structural — it determines who can invest and what you are entitled to know.

[VISUAL-HERO-01]

---

## Quick Answer (60 seconds)

Most fractional real estate platforms operate under either Regulation A (typically Tier 2, allowing up to $75 million per 12 months and non-accredited investors with a 10% cap) or Regulation D (typically Rule 506(c), which has no offering-size limit but admits only accredited investors). The framework choice is structural: it determines who can invest, how much disclosure you receive, and what ongoing reporting the issuer must do. Reg A trades months of SEC qualification overhead for retail accessibility; Reg D trades public access for offering speed. Neither is "safer" — they serve different audiences and product structures.

**Stat cards:**
- **Reg A Tier 2** - Public access, $75M cap
- **Reg D 506(c)** - Accredited only, no cap
- **$1M / $200K** - Accredited net worth / income thresholds
- **Form 1-K** - Tier 2 annual report (public on EDGAR)

## Opening

The first time it happens is the moment of confusion. You see a fractional real estate offering on one platform and you can invest a few hundred dollars. You see another offering on a different platform and the platform asks if you are an accredited investor — and tells you that you cannot invest if you are not. Same asset class, similar pitch, two different gates. The reason is structural and it traces back to which SEC exemption the offering is using to legally raise capital from people who are not professional investors.

This guide walks through the two frameworks most fractional real estate platforms use — Regulation A and Regulation D — from the investor's side of the desk. After reading, you should be able to tell which framework a given offering uses, why the platform chose it, and what you are entitled to as an investor under that framework. None of this is a recommendation that one framework is better. They serve different product structures and different investor segments.

## What is Regulation A (and what are its tiers)?

**Answer capsule:** Regulation A is an SEC exemption that lets companies raise capital from the public, including non-accredited investors. It has two tiers: Tier 1 allows up to $20 million per 12-month period; Tier 2 allows up to $75 million and supersedes state-level "blue sky" review, but adds ongoing SEC reporting requirements. Most consumer-facing fractional real estate platforms operate under Tier 2.

Regulation A was modernized by the JOBS Act of 2012 and the SEC's subsequent rulemaking (Regulation A+) in 2015. The Tier 2 offering cap was raised from $50 million to $75 million in March 2021. The framework is sometimes called "mini-IPO" because it requires SEC qualification of a public offering circular, but the disclosure and audit burdens sit below those of a full registered IPO.

The two tiers have meaningfully different mechanics:

- **Tier 1** allows offerings up to $20 million per rolling 12-month period. Financial statements need not be audited. The offering circular is reviewed by SEC staff AND by the securities regulator in each state where the offering is sold ("blue sky" review). There is no ongoing reporting requirement beyond a final report on the status of the offering.
- **Tier 2** allows offerings up to $75 million per rolling 12-month period. Financial statements MUST be audited by an independent accountant. The offering circular is reviewed only by SEC staff — state-level blue sky qualification is preempted. The issuer must file ongoing reports: Form 1-K (annual), Form 1-SA (semiannual), and Form 1-U (current report for material events). These reports are publicly accessible on EDGAR.

Tier 2 is the path most consumer-facing fractional platforms use. The combination of state-preemption (one SEC qualification serves all 50 states) and the higher offering cap fits the operating model of a platform that wants to admit non-accredited investors at scale.

## What is Regulation D (and what is the 506(b) vs 506(c) distinction)?

**Answer capsule:** Regulation D is an SEC exemption for private placements with no offering-size limit, but with strict investor restrictions. Rule 506(b) allows up to 35 sophisticated non-accredited investors plus unlimited accredited investors, with no general solicitation. Rule 506(c) allows unlimited accredited investors with verified accreditation, and DOES permit general solicitation. Many commercial real estate syndications use 506(c).

Reg D is the path most private placements take, because there is no SEC qualification process — the issuer files Form D within 15 days of the first sale and the offering is allowed to proceed. The trade-off is that the issuer cannot generally market the offering to the public unless they use Rule 506(c) and meet the verification requirements.

The two relevant rules under Reg D are 506(b) and 506(c):

- **Rule 506(b)** is the older path. The issuer can sell to an unlimited number of accredited investors and up to 35 sophisticated non-accredited investors who can evaluate the merits and risks. The issuer cannot use general solicitation or advertising — no public marketing, no website ads, no broad email campaigns. Communications are limited to pre-existing relationships. For an accredited investor under 506(b), the issuer needs only a "reasonable belief" of accreditation, often from a subscription-agreement questionnaire.
- **Rule 506(c)** was added by the JOBS Act in 2013. It lets the issuer publicly advertise the offering — including online, on social media, at conferences — provided two conditions hold: ALL investors must be accredited, AND the issuer must take reasonable steps to VERIFY the accredited status (not just rely on a questionnaire). Many crowdfunded commercial real estate syndications operate under 506(c) because the public-marketing freedom is operationally useful.

In March 2025, the SEC clarified that issuers may rely on minimum investment amounts as a reasonable step to verify accredited status under 506(c) — at least $200,000 for natural persons and $1 million for legal entities. This is a substantial loosening of the verification burden.

## Who is an "accredited investor" and why does it matter?

**Answer capsule:** An accredited investor meets specific income or net worth thresholds defined in SEC Rule 501: net worth exceeding $1 million excluding the primary residence, or annual income exceeding $200,000 ($300,000 with a spouse or spousal equivalent) in each of the past two years. Since 2020, certain professional credentials (Series 7, 65, 82) also qualify. Reg D 506(c) and most syndications restrict access to accredited investors.

The "accredited investor" concept is the SEC's mechanism for deciding who can participate in private capital markets without the full disclosure protections of registered offerings. The assumption is that an investor meeting the thresholds can bear the loss and access the sophistication needed to evaluate private offerings.

The current pathways to accredited status:

- **Income.** $200,000 in each of the past two years individually, OR $300,000 combined with a spouse or spousal equivalent in each of the past two years. Both years must individually clear the threshold — one strong year preceded by a year below it does not qualify. The 2020 amendments added "spousal equivalent" — a cohabitant in a relationship generally equivalent to that of a spouse — which lets unmarried partners pool finances on the same basis as married couples.
- **Net worth.** More than $1 million, alone or together with a spouse or spousal equivalent, excluding the value of the primary residence (and excluding mortgage debt secured by the primary residence up to the value of that residence).
- **Professional credentials.** Since 2020, holders of Series 7 (General Securities Representative), Series 65 (Investment Adviser Representative), or Series 82 (Private Securities Offerings Representative) licenses are accredited regardless of income or net worth. This pathway is underused because most retail investors don't have these licenses, but it matters for financial professionals.

Under 506(c), the issuer must verify your accreditation. Acceptable verification methods include reviewing tax forms (W-2, 1099, Schedule K-1 of Form 1065, or Form 1040), brokerage and bank statements dated within the prior 90 days, or a written confirmation from a registered broker-dealer, an SEC-registered investment adviser, a licensed attorney, or a certified public accountant who has independently verified your status.

A practical note on the net worth calculation: many readers conclude they are accredited because their home equity puts them over $1 million. The rule explicitly excludes the value of the primary residence (and the mortgage debt secured by the residence, up to the value of the residence). If your $1 million net worth depends on home equity, you are likely NOT accredited for SEC purposes — confirm with the platform or your CPA before assuming you qualify.

## What investor protections come with each framework?

**Answer capsule:** Reg A requires SEC qualification of an offering circular before sales, with disclosures of business, financials (audited for Tier 2), risk factors, and ongoing semiannual reporting on EDGAR. Reg D requires only Form D filing with no SEC qualification; disclosures sit in a private placement memorandum delivered to accredited investors. Reg A trades qualification overhead for public accessibility and transparency; Reg D assumes accredited-investor sophistication and forgoes the public review.

The disclosure profiles are different in kind, not just in degree.

**Under Reg A Tier 2**, the issuer must file an offering circular on Form 1-A that the SEC staff reviews and qualifies. The circular discloses the business, the offering structure, financial statements (audited), risk factors, use of proceeds, and other material information. Once qualified, the circular is publicly available on EDGAR. After the offering opens, the issuer files Form 1-K (annual report with audited financials), Form 1-SA (semiannual report), and Form 1-U (current report for material events). All of these are public on EDGAR. As an investor, you can review the offering circular, the most recent annual and semiannual reports, and any current-report filings before you commit capital, AND you receive ongoing disclosure for as long as you hold.

**Under Reg D**, the issuer files Form D (a short notice filing) within 15 days of the first sale. There is no SEC qualification; the offering opens without pre-sale review. Disclosures are typically delivered in a private placement memorandum (PPM) to accredited investors. The PPM is not public — only the investors in the offering see it. There is no ongoing reporting analogous to Tier 2's Form 1-K / 1-SA / 1-U; the issuer's disclosure obligations after closing run only to the investors who purchased.

Neither framework eliminates risk. Reg A gives you more public-record information up front and during the hold; Reg D gives the issuer faster time-to-market and assumes you are sophisticated enough to handle reduced disclosures. The right framework for an investor depends on whether you prefer transparency and broader accessibility (Reg A) or are eligible for and comfortable with private-placement disclosure norms (Reg D). The asset, the operator, and the terms drive returns more than the exemption — the framework is the legal structure inside which a specific deal lives or dies on its own merits.

## How do offering caps and timing differ?

**Answer capsule:** Reg A caps a rolling 12-month offering at $20 million (Tier 1) or $75 million (Tier 2). Reg D has no offering-amount limit. Reg A requires SEC qualification — typically months. Reg D needs only a Form D filing after the first sale. For a fractional platform, Reg D is faster to open but limits the investor pool; Reg A is slower but admits everyone.

The mechanics matter for what kinds of products each framework supports.

**Reg A** is a process. The offering circular goes to the SEC, staff reviews it (often multiple rounds of comments), and the issuer addresses comments before qualification. The process typically takes a few months for a first-time issuer, less for a platform that has done it before and is qualifying a similar follow-on offering. The 12-month rolling cap means a platform can run multiple offerings serially, each up to the cap, but cannot exceed $75 million in any 12-month window for any single offering vehicle.

**Reg D** is fast. The Form D notice is filed after the first sale, not before. The offering can technically open within days of the issuer finishing its PPM and subscription documents. The trade-off is the accredited-only restriction (under 506(c)) or the 35-non-accredited limit (under 506(b)), and no general public marketing under 506(b).

For a fractional real estate platform, the calculus often runs like this:

- A platform that wants to admit non-accredited retail investors at scale (think Fundrise, Arrived, Ark7, Realbricks, psfnetwork) chooses Reg A Tier 2. The SEC qualification overhead is real, but it unlocks the entire retail investor base and preempts state-by-state qualification.
- A platform that targets accredited investors with larger property-by-property offerings (think CrowdStreet's larger deals, many commercial syndications) chooses Reg D 506(c). The verification overhead is now lower after the March 2025 minimum-investment guidance, and the no-cap offering size accommodates larger deals.

## Which framework do fractional real estate platforms typically use?

**Answer capsule:** Most consumer-facing fractional platforms operate under Regulation A Tier 2, because Tier 2 admits non-accredited investors with a per-offering cap of 10% of the greater of annual income or net worth (excluding primary residence). Larger commercial real estate syndications often use Reg D 506(c) when their target investors are accredited and the offering size exceeds the Reg A Tier 2 cap or speed-to-market matters.

The reader-visible result is the gate at the platform sign-up:

- **No accreditation gate, "anyone can invest" framing:** the platform is almost certainly running under Reg A Tier 2. Examples: Fundrise's eREITs and eFunds (Reg A), Arrived's single-family rental offerings (Reg A), Realbricks (Reg A), Ark7 (Reg A), psfnetwork (Reg A).
- **Accreditation gate at sign-up, "verify your accredited status" flow:** the platform is running under Reg D 506(c). Examples: CrowdStreet's deal-by-deal offerings to accredited investors, most commercial syndications listed on platforms like RealCrowd or EquityMultiple's larger deals.
- **Hybrid:** some platforms have historically run both — Reg A for retail offerings and Reg D for accredited-only larger deals. The platform's offering page typically labels each one, so the framework is visible per offering rather than per platform.

The framework choice is not hidden. The legal page or offering circular section of any platform's site discloses which exemption is being used. If you cannot find it on the public site, the offering circular (Reg A) or PPM (Reg D) will state it on the first few pages. Make this a routine due-diligence step before investing.

## How does this apply to psfnetwork specifically?

**Answer capsule:** psfnetwork operates under Regulation A Tier 2. The offering circular for each property is qualified by the SEC and publicly filed on EDGAR; non-accredited investors can participate subject to the 10% cap on the greater of annual income or net worth (excluding primary residence). The per-square-foot model is a unit-of-ownership detail — it doesn't change the regulatory framework. The framework is what enables the consumer-facing model.

Under Reg A Tier 2, every psfnetwork property offering produces a publicly accessible paper trail: the qualified offering circular, the annual Form 1-K with audited financials, the semiannual Form 1-SA, and Form 1-U filings for material events. These are searchable on EDGAR by issuer name. Before investing in a specific property, look up its filings and read the offering circular's risk factors and operating-agreement excerpts.

Why Reg A Tier 2 specifically? Three reasons mirror the broader industry pattern. First, retail accessibility is the entire premise of fractional ownership — the framework choice has to admit non-accredited investors, which rules out Reg D 506(c). Second, state-level preemption matters when you operate across all 50 states; Reg A Tier 2 gives you that, Reg A Tier 1 does not. Third, the offering cap fits the model — a single property at $75 million is a comfortable ceiling for the size of property a fractional platform typically lists.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I invest in a Reg D 506(c) offering if I'm not accredited?**
A: No. Rule 506(c) limits sales to accredited investors only, and the issuer must take reasonable steps to verify your accredited status. If a platform is offering under 506(c) and you do not meet the income, net worth, or credentialed-professional thresholds, you cannot legally participate. If you want to invest in private real estate without being accredited, the path is Reg A (Tier 1 or Tier 2) offerings or, for very small rounds, Reg CF (crowdfunding under the JOBS Act).

**Q: What does "general solicitation" mean and why does it matter?**
A: General solicitation is broad public marketing of a securities offering — website ads, social media posts, conference presentations, mass emails to people you don't know. Rule 506(b) prohibits general solicitation; the issuer can only sell to people with whom there is a substantive pre-existing relationship. Rule 506(c) permits general solicitation but adds the accreditation verification requirement. Reg A permits general solicitation because the offering circular has been SEC-qualified.

**Q: Are Reg A offerings safer than Reg D?**
A: Neither framework is "safer" — they serve different audiences. Reg A gives you more public-record disclosure and ongoing reporting; Reg D gives the issuer faster time-to-market and assumes accredited-investor sophistication. Both can produce excellent and terrible offerings. Read the disclosures, evaluate the operator, and consider the underlying asset on its own merits regardless of framework.

**Q: How do I check what framework a platform uses?**
A: The platform's legal page or "About" section usually states it. For specific offerings, the offering circular (Reg A) or PPM (Reg D) names the exemption on the cover page. You can also search EDGAR (https://efts.sec.gov) for the issuer name — Reg A filings appear there as Form 1-A, 1-K, 1-SA, 1-U; Reg D filings appear as Form D notices.

**Q: Can a fractional platform use both Reg A and Reg D?**
A: Yes. Different offerings on the same platform can use different exemptions. A platform might run its retail-focused property offerings under Reg A Tier 2 and a parallel accredited-only fund or larger commercial deal under Reg D 506(c). Each offering's specific exemption is named in its offering documents.

**Q: What is Reg CF and how does it compare?**
A: Regulation Crowdfunding (Reg CF), created by the JOBS Act of 2012, allows companies to raise up to $5 million per 12-month period from accredited AND non-accredited investors through SEC-registered intermediary platforms. Reg CF imposes explicit per-investor caps based on the investor's income and net worth — these caps are designed as retail-investor protections, distinct from Reg A Tier 2's per-offering 10% rule (which limits any single offering but does not aggregate across multiple Reg A offerings). Reg CF sits below Reg A in offering size and above the smallest Reg D rounds in retail accessibility. Real estate use is less common than Reg A Tier 2 for fractional platforms because the $5M cap is restrictive for property-by-property offerings.
