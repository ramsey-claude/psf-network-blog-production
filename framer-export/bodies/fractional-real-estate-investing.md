## What is fractional real estate investing?

Fractional real estate investing is the practice of multiple investors co-owning a single property through shares in a special purpose entity, usually an LLC, that holds title. Each investor receives a proportional share of rental income and appreciation. Online platforms standardized the model in the US after the JOBS Act of 2012, a federal law that loosened the rules on who companies could raise money from, which for the first time let these deals be advertised to non-accredited (everyday) investors. Minimums now start as low as $20.

The idea is not new. Wealthy families have co-owned vacation homes and commercial buildings for decades through partnerships and trusts. What changed is access. Online platforms standardized the legal structure, lowered the minimums, and used Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) exemptions to allow public marketing of these offerings. The result is a category that now holds billions in assets across major US platforms like Fundrise, Arrived, Ark7, and PSFnetwork, and it continues to grow.

When you buy fractional shares, you are not buying a deed. You are buying a security, typically a unit of membership interest in an LLC that owns the property. That distinction matters for taxes, for how you can exit, and for what happens if the platform itself runs into trouble. This is the part of fractional property ownership that newcomers most often misunderstand.

## How does fractional real estate investing work?

Most fractional real estate platforms follow a four-step process. The platform sources a property, places it in an LLC, divides the LLC into shares, and sells those shares through an SEC-qualified offering. Once funded, the platform manages the property, collects rent, and distributes net income to investors monthly or quarterly. When the property is sold, proceeds are distributed in proportion to ownership.

Step 1 is property sourcing. The platform identifies a property, performs due diligence, and prices it. Some platforms focus on single-family rentals, others on small multifamily, and a few on commercial real estate.

Step 2 is securitization. The property is placed inside an LLC, which issues shares or membership units. The offering is filed with the SEC under one of two main frameworks: Regulation A, which can be marketed publicly and accepts non-accredited investors, capped at $75 million per 12 months under Tier 2, or Regulation D, which is typically limited to accredited investors.

Step 3 is funding. Investors buy shares. Minimums vary by platform, from $20 on some to $100 or $5,000 on others. Most platforms require investors to be at least 18 and to have a US bank account. Under Regulation A Tier 2, a non-accredited investor may invest no more than 10 percent of the greater of their annual income or net worth in a single offering, so size your position with that cap in mind.

Step 4 is distributions. The platform manages the property, collects rent, pays expenses, and distributes net income. For US investors, distributions are typically reported on a Schedule K-1 (Form 1065) because the LLC is usually a pass-through entity. K-1 reporting can be more complex than a standard 1099 and may delay your tax filing, so consult a tax advisor before investing. When the property is sold, proceeds are distributed in proportion to ownership after any platform fees or carry. Returns are not guaranteed at any step: property values can fall, tenants can default, and platforms can change their fee structures.

## How does fractional ownership compare to owning a whole property?

Fractional ownership lowers the entry point and removes the operational burden, but in exchange you give up direct control, accept reduced liquidity, and pay platform fees. Owning a whole property gives you full control and full upside, but it requires a large down payment, ongoing management work, and significant concentration risk in a single asset.

| **Dimension** | **Fractional** | **Whole property** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Minimum capital | $20 to $5,000 | $20,000+ down payment typical |
| Control | None over property decisions | Full |
| Effort | Passive | Active, or hire a manager |
| Liquidity | Limited, platform-dependent | Sell the property to exit |
| Tax treatment | K-1 from LLC, pass-through | Direct ownership, depreciation deductions |
| Diversification | Easy across many properties | Hard, capital-intensive |
| Risk concentration | Spread across properties | Concentrated in one asset |

The fractional model also splits along platform structure. Some platforms use a per-share model, where each share represents a fixed dollar amount. Others, like PSFnetwork, use a per-square-foot model, where each unit of ownership maps to a square foot of the property. Both are securities under SEC frameworks, so the difference is presentation, not the underlying legal structure. For anyone comparing options, this is where the question of what is fractional ownership stops being abstract and starts being a concrete choice between unit types.

## What is the square-foot ownership model?

The square-foot ownership model expresses your stake as a number of square feet rather than shares. You still hold an LLC membership interest, but the unit of ownership maps to the physical property. If a property is 1,200 square feet priced at $400 per square foot, then 50 square feet costs $20,000 and represents 4.17 percent of the property and its income.

These figures are for illustration only. Actual property pricing varies, and the security itself remains an LLC membership interest, simply priced per square foot. The square-foot model is a presentation layer on top of the same LLC-and-shares structure other platforms use. It does not change the underlying securities law treatment, and the same risk disclosures apply. What it changes is intuition. Most people have a sense of how much 100 square feet is and what rent it might generate, but few have that intuition for a share that costs $73.21. That tangibility is the entire appeal, and it sits closer to how people already think about fractional home ownership than an abstract share count does.

## Is fractional real estate a good investment?

Fractional real estate can be a useful addition to a diversified portfolio for investors who want real estate exposure, accept illiquidity, and have a multi-year time horizon. It is not a substitute for an emergency fund, a retirement account, or any short-horizon investment. So is fractional real estate a good investment for you? Platform-reported annual dividend yields have historically ranged from 4 to 9 percent, with additional return potential from appreciation, but these figures are not guarantees and past performance does not predict future results.

The case for it is straightforward: lower minimums than direct ownership, passive income, property value appreciation, exposure to a different asset class than stocks and bonds, and a structure that handles management for you. The case against it is just as real: limited liquidity, since most platforms do not have a deep secondary market; platform risk, because the platform itself can fail; fee drag, as annual management fees, sourcing fees, and carry can compress returns; and concentration risk inside the platform, because you depend on its underwriting.

Comparing returns across fractional platforms, public REITs, and direct rental ownership takes care. Platform-reported figures, REIT index returns, and direct-rental ROI surveys all use different fee, vintage, and survivorship assumptions, so a like-for-like comparison is rare, and any single number for "fractional return" or "REIT return" understates the variance. Use ranges, read the fine print, and treat any specific historical number as backward-looking.

The model suits an investor who has already covered emergency savings and high-interest debt, can leave the capital invested for five or more years, wants real estate exposure without becoming a landlord, and understands that principal loss is possible. It does not suit an investor who needs the capital back within 12 months, expects guaranteed returns (no investment offers those), or is uncomfortable holding LLC membership interests.

## How do you choose a fractional real estate platform?

Choose a platform on five criteria: regulatory status, fee structure, liquidity terms, property mix, and distribution frequency. Regulatory status matters most, because it determines what disclosures you receive and what investor protections apply to you. Fees compound, so even a 1 percent annual difference can erode multi-year returns. Liquidity terms vary widely, and some platforms lock you in for the full property hold of five or more years.

First, regulatory status. Confirm the platform files offerings under SEC Regulation A, open to non-accredited investors with disclosure, or Regulation D, typically accredited investors only. Verify the issuing entity in SEC EDGAR. Under Regulation A Tier 2, non-accredited investors are capped at 10 percent of the greater of their annual income or net worth per offering. Regulation D Rule 506(b) allows up to 35 non-accredited but sophisticated investors plus unlimited accredited ones, with no general solicitation, while Rule 506(c) allows general solicitation but accepts only accredited investors. Avoid platforms with no US regulatory filings.

Second, fee structure. Look at three layers: a sourcing or acquisition fee paid upfront, an annual management fee often in the 1 to 2 percent range, and any carried interest or performance fee on appreciation. Calculate the all-in fee load before you invest.

Third, liquidity terms. Read the operating agreement. Some platforms offer a secondary market for shares; others lock you in until the property is sold, often in five to ten years. Some offer redemption windows and many do not. PSFnetwork, for example, has no current secondary market, so shares may be difficult or impossible to sell before the property is sold, and you should be prepared to hold for the full term.

Fourth, property mix. Decide whether you want single-family rentals, multifamily, commercial, or a mix. Single-family is simpler, multifamily often has steadier income, and commercial carries the highest variance.

Fifth, distribution frequency. Monthly distributions feel better psychologically but do not change total returns. Quarterly is more common and reduces administrative friction.

A defensible due diligence process pulls the SEC filing for the specific offering, reads the risk factors section in full, compares fee schedules across at least three platforms, and checks the platform's track record across full property cycles, not just current vintages.

## FAQ

### Q: Is fractional real estate investing safe?

A: It is not risk-free. The principal risks are loss of principal if property values decline, illiquidity if you need to exit early, and platform risk if the operator fails. Regulated platforms must disclose these risks in their offering documents. Past performance is not indicative of future results, and returns are not guaranteed.

### Q: How is income from fractional real estate taxed?

A: For US investors, income is generally reported on a Schedule K-1 (Form 1065) because most platforms use pass-through LLC structures. You may also receive depreciation pass-through, which can offset some of the distribution income. Tax treatment varies by structure and by jurisdiction, so consult a tax advisor before investing.

### Q: What happens if the platform shuts down?

A: The property is held in an LLC separate from the platform itself, so a platform shutdown does not automatically wipe out the underlying property value. Finding a replacement servicer can take time, though, and disputes can take months or years to resolve, so full recovery is not assured. Read the operating agreement's backup-servicer language before investing, not after.

### Q: How is fractional real estate different from a REIT?

A: A REIT is a publicly traded or non-traded entity that holds a portfolio of properties and, by tax rule, distributes at least 90 percent of taxable income to shareholders as dividends. A fractional real estate investment is typically a stake in a single property or a small portfolio held by an LLC. Publicly traded REITs trade daily and offer broad sector exposure, while fractional investing lets you pick a specific property but offers less liquidity.

### Q: What is the minimum investment for fractional real estate?

A: Minimums vary by platform. Some regulated US platforms offer minimums as low as $20, others start at $100 or $5,000, and PSFnetwork sells by the square foot rather than a flat dollar minimum. Regulation D offerings typically require accredited investor status, which has its own income or net worth thresholds.

### Q: Are fractional real estate investments FDIC insured?

A: No. FDIC deposit insurance covers depositor accounts at insured banks, up to $250,000 per depositor per insured bank per ownership category. Fractional real estate investments are securities, specifically LLC membership interests, not deposits, and are not insured by the FDIC or any government agency.

## Conclusion

Fractional real estate investing is best understood as a trade. You hand over control, liquidity, and a slice of your return in fees, and in exchange you get into an asset class that used to demand a five-figure down payment and a second job as a landlord. For the right investor, that trade is worth making. The model rewards people who already have their emergency fund and high-interest debt handled, who can leave the money alone for five years or more, and who treat it as one diversified slice of a portfolio rather than a single big bet. The mechanics are not complicated once you see them clearly: you buy a security, usually an LLC membership interest, that pays you a proportional share of rent and appreciation, whether that share is counted in shares or, on a platform like PSFnetwork, in square feet. The discipline is all in the due diligence. Verify the SEC filing, read the risk factors and the operating agreement in full, compare the all-in fees across several platforms, and respect the multi-year hold. Do that, accept that principal loss is genuinely possible, and fractional real estate can be a durable, hands-off way to own property. Skip it, and the low minimum just lowers the cost of a mistake.
