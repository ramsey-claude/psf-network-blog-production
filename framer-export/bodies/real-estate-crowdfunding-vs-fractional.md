## What is real estate crowdfunding?

Real estate crowdfunding is a financing mechanism in which many small investors pool capital to fund a real estate deal. It operates under one of three SEC frameworks: Regulation Crowdfunding (Reg CF), where Reg CF real estate offerings can raise up to $5 million per 12 months with investor-level caps; Regulation A, whose Tier 2 allows up to $75 million per 12 months and admits non-accredited investors; or Regulation D, which is typically limited to accredited investors. The pooling is the mechanism. What sits underneath can be either equity in a property entity or debt to a property project.

The category grew out of the JOBS Act of 2012, which created Regulation Crowdfunding and modernized Regulation A. Before that, raising real estate capital from non-accredited investors meant either a public offering, which was expensive, or a private placement, which capped the number of investors. Crowdfunding real estate investing made small-dollar pooling legal at scale, and platforms such as Fundrise, Groundfloor on the debt side, and CrowdStreet on the commercial side built on it, alongside many fractional platforms whose offerings are crowdfunded under Reg A.

## What is fractional real estate?

Fractional real estate refers specifically to equity ownership of a property divided into shares. You buy an LLC membership interest in the entity that owns the property and receive a proportional share of rental income, plus any appreciation when the property sells. Income flows through to you and is reported on a K-1 (a tax form used by pass-through investment entities). Platforms include Fundrise, Arrived, Ark7, Realbricks, and PSFnetwork. The legal structure is consistent: a pass-through LLC issuing shares, or in PSFnetwork's case square feet, under SEC Regulation A or D.

Fractional is really a description of the product rather than the financing. The money is usually raised through crowdfunding, many small investors pooling capital, but the framing is that you own a piece of this specific property rather than that you funded this project.

## Where the two overlap

The two overlap most in their mechanics. Most equity fractional platforms run their offerings under Regulation A, the same framework many crowdfunding platforms use, so the investor experience is largely identical: open an account, pass KYC, browse offerings, fund the account, buy shares. The legal entity (an LLC), the tax form (a K-1), and the regulatory disclosures line up closely.

In practice, a Fundrise eREIT and a single-property offering on Arrived are both crowdfunded under SEC frameworks. The investor is taking part in a crowdfunded offering either way. The marketing labels differ; the regulatory mechanism is often the same.

## Where they actually differ

A few differences do real work. The first is debt versus equity: real estate crowdfunding can be debt-side, where you lend to a project, earn interest, and are repaid at maturity, which fractional is not. The second is single-property versus multi-property: fractional typically gives you a stake in one specific property, while many crowdfunding products, such as Fundrise's funds, pool many properties into a single offering. The third is hold structure and exit: debt crowdfunding can carry a defined maturity, often 12 to 36 months on a platform like Groundfloor, whereas fractional equity is usually open-ended.

## Matching the structure to the investor

Debt crowdfunding, on Groundfloor and similar platforms, tends to suit income-focused investors who want a defined maturity and interest income. Fund-style equity crowdfunding, such as Fundrise, suits investors who want diversified property exposure managed at the fund level. Single-property fractional, on Arrived, Ark7, Realbricks, or PSFnetwork, suits investors who want to choose the specific property and collect passive income from it. The deciding factor is usually what you want most: scheduled interest with a set maturity (debt), broad diversification (a fund), or passive income from a property you picked yourself (single-property fractional).

For many investors building a real estate allocation from scratch, a blend is reasonable: a smaller debt position for income, a larger equity position for appreciation exposure, with the equity split between fund-style (Fundrise) and single-property fractional (Arrived or PSFnetwork, which sells mortgage-free property by the square foot) depending on whether you enjoy picking properties or prefer a set-and-forget approach.

## FAQ

### Q: Is Fundrise crowdfunding or fractional?

A: Both. Fundrise runs eREITs, its branded fund-style real estate products, that are crowdfunded under SEC frameworks. The funds hold many properties, so you own shares of the fund rather than of one specific property. In common usage Fundrise is called crowdfunding more often than fractional, though the legal mechanism overlaps with fractional.

### Q: Is fractional real estate the same as REIT crowdfunding?

A: Not exactly. A REIT is a specific legal and tax entity that must distribute at least 90 percent of taxable income and meet asset-test rules. Fractional platforms typically use LLC structures rather than REITs. Fundrise's eREITs are REITs that are also crowdfunded, while single-property fractional platforms are usually LLCs, not REITs.

### Q: What is Reg CF?

A: Regulation Crowdfunding, an SEC framework created by the JOBS Act of 2012. It lets companies raise up to $5 million per 12 months from both accredited and non-accredited investors through SEC-registered intermediaries. Non-accredited investors face limits on how much they can invest, based on income and net worth.

### Q: Which is safer, crowdfunding or fractional?

A: Neither is risk-free, and the honest answer turns on the structure rather than the label. Debt crowdfunding offers a defined maturity and interest payments but carries borrower-default risk. Equity fractional has an open-ended hold and participates in appreciation, with its own market and platform risk. Past performance does not predict future results.

### Q: Can the same offering be both crowdfunding and fractional?

A: Yes, and most equity fractional offerings are crowdfunded under Reg A or Reg D. The two labels describe different aspects of the same product: crowdfunded describes how it was financed, fractional describes what you own.

The confusion between these two terms is understandable, because on most platforms the same offering is accurately described by both. Where the words stop being interchangeable is at the edges that the marketing rarely mentions, since crowdfunding stretches to cover debt positions and multi-property funds that fractional, by definition, does not.

For an investor, the label matters less than the structure underneath it. Read the offering documents, confirm the regulatory framework, and match the structure to the role you want real estate to play in your portfolio.
